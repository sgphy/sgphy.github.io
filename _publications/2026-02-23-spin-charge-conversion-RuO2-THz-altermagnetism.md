Confirming altermagnetism in RuO₂ requires precise understanding of mechanisms governing its spin transport. Using ultrafast terahertz emission spectroscopy on epitaxial thin films of the altermagnetic candidate RuO₂, we investigate spin-to-charge current conversion and perform a quantitative analysis of competing effects contributing to the measured anisotropic THz emission. These include the anisotropic inverse spin splitter and spin Hall effects in RuO₂, the anisotropic conductivity of RuO₂, and the birefringence of the TiO₂ substrate. We find that the leading contribution to the measured signals comes from the anisotropic inverse spin Hall effect, with an average spin-Hall angle of 2.4 × 10⁻³ at room temperature. In contrast, a possible contribution from the altermagnetic inverse spin-splitter effect is found to be significantly smaller. This work provides a rigorous benchmark for the field, emphasizing the necessity of carefully distinguishing relativistic spin-orbit coupling effects from signatures of unconventional magnetic order.
